2030

The decade the archipelago didn’t see coming.

An AI-era scenario of Philippine economic collapse — how the BPO sector, the remittance base, and the peso fail together between 2026 and 2032, and what a country of 110 million looks like at the end of it. Every number inside is drawn from the research dossier. Every scene is composite fiction anchored to a dated fact.

How to read this. Each chapter is a year. Each year is one scene, one family, one set of numbers. The sidebar tracks the scenario as it unfolds. This path carries a ~21% probability — the second most likely outcome, behind the Accelerated scenario at 66%. The Optimistic managed transition, at 13%, is the actual tail.

Chapter 01 · Late 2026

2026

The first tremors arrive in a single quarterly earnings call — and almost no one in Manila notices, because almost no one in Manila is listening to a Concentrix earnings call at 5:30 in the morning. The country is already in a declared national energy emergency. The jeepney fare to Ortigas has risen 34% since February. Nobody has bandwidth for a Concentrix earnings call.

Opening scene · Concentrix, Ortigas · Tuesday, 29 September 2026, 6:14 a.m.

Maria Villanueva is finishing her graveyard shift on the 23rd floor of Robinsons Cyberscape Gamma when the phone at her workstation chirps twice — an internal Teams notification, not a customer call — and the floor goes very quiet in the particular way floors go quiet when 340 people all read the same message at the same second.

She is twenty-nine years old. She is team lead for the St. Louis-based healthcare account they all call, for reasons no one remembers, “Bluebird.” She has been at the same desk, with minor promotions, since 2019. She has two kids — Elijah is seven, Sophia is four — and a one-bedroom at SMDC Light Residences in Mandaluyong she is paying off on a five-year plan at ₱31,400 a month. Her English sounds, to an American ear, vaguely Midwestern. She learned it at a call center, from the calls themselves.

The message from Bluebird’s vendor-management office is three sentences. Effective 1 January 2027, all tier-1 inbound voice support for the member-services line will transition to an AI-native provider. Concentrix will retain tier-2 escalations, complex claims, and Spanish-language work. A transition plan will be shared by the account leadership in the coming week.

Her supervisor, Joseph, stands up at his desk two rows away. He does not say anything. He just looks at her, and then at the ceiling, and then sits back down. On the whiteboard behind him, last week’s CSAT target — 88% — is still written in blue dry-erase marker. They had hit 91.1

Two shocks, one country

On 25 March 2026, six months before Maria’s floor went quiet, President Marcos signed Executive Order 110 — a state of national energy emergency, declared in the weeks following the Iranian war and the Strait of Hormuz closure. The Philippines imports 98% of its oil from the Middle East. Diesel crossed ₱130 a liter. Four hundred and twenty-five filling stations closed. The jeepney operators got a provisional fare increase. The peso, already under pressure from oil-import costs, broke ₱60 in mid-March for the first time in BSP recorded history — a milestone the Monetary Board had twice named as its informal defense line — before a $1.4-billion intervention held it temporarily. The BSP was already stretched before the BPO wave arrived. The country entered the AI displacement shock not from a position of macroeconomic stability but from a declared emergency. This is the condition in which the Bluebird message lands.

The contraction had been visible in the data for fifteen months. Philippine services exports fell 4.2% year-on-year in Q2 2025 — the first non-COVID contraction since 2012 — before anyone on Maria’s floor had heard the phrase “agentic deployment.” By the time Concentrix took a $1.52 billion goodwill impairment on the Webhelp book in the fourth quarter, IBPAP had already issued a statement reassuring members that the sector remained on track for its $59 billion, 2.5 million workers by 2028 target. No one at IBPAP explained how the arithmetic closed. No one in the Senate asked.

On the jeepney home — the fare is ₱18 now, up from ₱13 in January, since diesel crossed ₱130 a liter in March and the LTFRB allowed operators a provisional increase — Maria scrolls past a Rappler push alert: TaskUs taken private by Blackstone and founders in all-cash $2.1B deal. She swipes it away without opening it. She is thinking about the ₱31,400. She is thinking about her brother Benjie’s contract in Riyadh, which is up for renewal in March, in a city that is six months into a war that has made the Strait of Hormuz impassable and made Benjie’s employer very, very quiet about the renewal. She is thinking about whether she can skip buying Elijah the school shoes until payday after next.

The arithmetic problem, stated plainly

IBPAP’s 2028 target assumes $59B revenue on 2.5M workers with rising revenue per employee. You cannot have all three. The industry’s own AI strategy — deflection, outcome-based pricing, revenue-per-employee growth — makes rising headcount arithmetically incompatible with the stated target. Two of the three numbers have to give. In late 2026, no one in Philippine public life is saying which two.

That evening, the Bluebird account manager in St. Louis — a woman named Diane who Maria has spoken to twice on video — sends a personal note. It is kind. It uses the word difficult twice. It explains that the decision was made at the parent company level, over the objection of the operational team, because the vendor’s pilot had come back at 73% deflection on tier-1 queries with a CSAT of 4.4 against Concentrix’s 4.2. “It isn’t about you,” Diane writes, and Maria believes her, and it doesn’t matter.

On the group chat with the other team leads, someone posts a screenshot of a Klarna press release from 2024 — the one where the CEO had walked back full AI deployment and rehired human agents. See, the message reads, it doesn’t actually work. One of the senior leads, Marvin, replies within the minute. Read it again. They cut 700 of their 850 FTEs and we’re passing the article around like they hired them back. Seven hundred. The 150 is the new permanent headcount. No one responds. The chat goes quiet for eleven minutes and then someone changes the subject to Sunday’s PBA game.

By the close of 2026, roughly 42,000 tier-1 voice seats have been quietly moved off Philippine rosters. The figure will not appear in any official release until the 2027 IBPAP annual report, by which time the number will be larger by an order of magnitude.
Chapter 01 · The first tremors

In the research dossier, this year is a single downward tick on a chart that does not yet look alarming. Figure 01 shows all three scenario paths still clustered within a few percentage points of each other through the end of 2026. The divergence begins in 2027. The chart is, in this sense, a polite chart. The floors it represents are not polite.

Maria gets home at 7:40 a.m. Her mother is already on the jeep to drop Sophia at daycare. Elijah is at the table, in uniform, eating pandesal with his hands and watching a tablet. The apartment smells of Nescafé and warm bread from the bakery downstairs. Maria kisses the top of his head, tells him she loves him, goes to the bedroom, closes the door, and does not cry. She sleeps until 2:30. When she wakes up, she opens her banking app and looks at the condo loan balance. ₱1,394,200 remaining. Thirty-seven months.

The first tremor has arrived. It is one account, on one floor, on one Tuesday. There are 1.9 million BPO workers in the country. The ground has moved under perhaps 340 of them. The news cycle will not pick this up for another fourteen weeks.

Chapter 02 · 2027

2027

The gradient becomes a cliff. By December, 280,000 seats are gone, office vacancies crack 30%, and the phrase “AI dividend” enters the vocabulary of every procurement officer in North America.

Scene · Dallas, 11 March 2027, 2:40 p.m. CST

The slide is number 23 of 47 in an internal deck at a Fortune 50 healthcare insurer whose name, for legal reasons, cannot be used here. The title reads: Q1 pilot outcomes — member services transformation. The numbers, against a green checkmark: 74% deflection at tier-1, CSAT 4.41 (baseline 4.19), AHT down 62%, cost per resolved contact $0.063 against the Concentrix contract rate of $5.80. The room is seven people. One of them is a VP of procurement named Andrea, who is forty-six, who has spent nineteen years managing BPO vendor relationships, and who understands on this slide that her job description is about to change and that several hundred thousand jobs in a country she has never visited are going to change with it.

Within ninety days, a version of this deck will circulate, under NDA, through the procurement offices of seventeen Fortune 500 firms. By September, eleven of them will have issued RFPs with outcome-based pricing floors in the $0.05–0.20 per resolved contact band. Every one of those RFPs will quietly ask vendors to certify an AI-to-human ratio by Q2 2028. The second wave of enterprise buyers stops waiting for conclusive evidence. The evidence has arrived.

Scene · Ortigas, Pasig, 14 May 2027, 9:20 p.m.

Maria and Joseph and two other team leads are at a 24-hour Jollibee on Julia Vargas, eating late because they had all been in the same eight-hour retrenchment meeting, and because no one wants to go home yet. The Chickenjoy on Maria’s tray has gone cold. Nobody has touched theirs since they sat down. Maria’s separation package is 1.5 months’ pay per year of service, plus accrued leave, plus a ₱50,000 “transition allowance” that the HR deck had described as generous and the lawyer they consulted had described as the legal minimum. It comes to ₱612,000. The condo balance is ₱1,244,000. She is doing arithmetic in her head that does not close.

Joseph is reading aloud from the IBPAP press release that had come out that morning. The sector remains resilient. Displacement is localized. Headcount targets for 2028 are under review but remain on-track directionally. Marvin, the senior lead from the 2026 group chat, says without looking up from his beer: Directionally. Nobody laughs. A GrabFood rider queue has formed on the sidewalk outside, longer than it used to be. Maria notices that three of the riders in the queue are wearing Concentrix lanyards they have forgotten to take off.

The Alliance of Call Center Agents triples its membership between May and October. Its strike fund — designed for a sector that had almost never needed to strike — runs dry by September. Eleven NLRC illegal-dismissal cases are filed in Metro Manila in August alone, more than the total for the preceding two years combined. The DOLE regional office on EDSA in Mandaluyong extends its walk-in hours to 8 p.m. to absorb the volume. The queue does not shorten. On a Tuesday in early October, it extends into the parking lot. Security is called twice. A GrabFood rider who has been waiting since 8 a.m. — still wearing a Concentrix lanyard, which has become, in 2027, a kind of informal badge — is told his case may take six to eight months to assess. He thanks the officer, goes back to the parking lot, and gets on his bike.

The aggregate number for 2027, released later, is 280,000 seats lost — just inside the 150–300K first-wave band the research had forecast. The composition is what mattered. Tier-1 voice (US accounts): −41%. Entry-level content moderation: −58%. Basic AP/invoice matching: −67%. Outbound telesales: effectively zero. The growth segments — global capability centers, analytics consulting, regulatory-mandated trust & safety — added 34,000 roles. The net was a hole the size of Iloilo’s entire BPO workforce, concentrated in Metro Manila and Cebu, realized over forty-one weeks.

The property market moves first, because the property market always moves first. Colliers’ Q3 2027 vacancy print lands at 31.4% in the Bay Area, 28.9% in Alabang, 24.1% overall Metro Manila. One hundred and ninety thousand square meters of new supply delivers into a market shrinking by seat count. Peripheral condo asking rents in Parañaque and Pasig fall 18% against 2025. Three of the largest developer-landlords issue covenant waivers to their anchor BPO tenants. The two smallest ones do not, and the quiet restructurings begin.

The second-order wave

For every BPO seat lost, roughly 0.8 indirect jobs vanish with it — drivers, canteen staff, security, the mall tenants who sold them lunch. The 280,000 direct seats of 2027 produce ~224,000 additional informal-sector losses. None of these workers qualify for the SSS one-time unemployment benefit. None of them appear in the IBPAP headline number.

In September, at a Senate committee hearing on the services-export contraction, the IBPAP representative is asked whether the 2028 target of 2.5 million jobs is still realistic. He pauses for four seconds — the clip will be shared 1.4 million times — and says: The target is aspirational. He is corrected within the hour by an IBPAP press office clarification stating that the target remains operative. By that point it no longer matters. The word aspirational is already in the evening news chyron. This is the sound a $32-billion industry makes when it admits, in a single word, on live television, that its own roadmap was a forecast of the weather it wanted.

Maria files for the SSS unemployment benefit in late May. It pays ₱22,000, once, and then nothing. Her mother sends ₱8,000 from Pangasinan out of the teacher’s-pension savings Maria had not known about. Benjie, in Riyadh, increases his monthly remittance from ₱22,000 to ₱28,000 and stops sending his girlfriend in Antipolo anything at all. Everyone is holding the household up at the same time. The arithmetic still does not close.

In late July, a GCC recruiter reaches her through LinkedIn — the profile update she had made quietly in June, not expecting anything. The role is real: a Taguig-based analytics-operations lead at ₱55,000 a month, 16% above her Concentrix salary, with the same health-benefit package she has been holding the household on. One condition: the team is relocating its operations center to Clark in the first quarter of 2028. Maria calculates this for four days. Elijah is in Grade 3 in Mandaluyong. Sophia is at daycare down the street. Nanay Tess is in Bayambang, fifty minutes from a hospital that takes PhilHealth. Benjie is in Riyadh. She would be alone with two children in a city she does not know, on a three-month probationary contract, ninety-five kilometers from every support structure the household runs on. She sends the recruiter a polite reply declining. She never learns that the GCC’s Clark operations are placed under review four months later — that by October 2028, the consolidation has shifted from Clark to Kuala Lumpur — that the door she chose not to walk through closes on its own, from the other side.

On 3 November, at the Ayala Triangle Christmas tree lighting, the crowd is markedly thinner than in 2025. Maria takes Elijah and Sophia anyway. Elijah asks why there are fewer people this year. She tells him she doesn’t know. She is telling him the truth and she is telling him a lie at the same time.

The displacement wave is not invisible. It is visible and legible and in the data. The Philippine state, in late 2027, has not yet decided whether to name it.

Chapter 03 · 2028

2028

Presidential election year. A five-way race with no runoff. The candidate polling at 11% in January wins with 34.7% in May. She is inaugurated on 30 June. By December the peso is at ₱66.40 and the one BPO segment that had been growing — global capability centers — is quietly evaluating Kuala Lumpur.

Scene · Smart Araneta Coliseum, Cubao, 9 March 2028, 7:18 p.m.

The senator is a second-term labor-rights lawyer from Cagayan de Oro, fifty-two years old — daughter of a Bukidnon sugarcane workers’ organizer who died in a 1998 labor dispute that went unreported in Manila because a typhoon made landfall the same week. She had spent eight years on the Senate Labor Committee before anyone outside Mindanao had heard of her. Her supporters call themselves Manggagawa Tayo — We Are Workers — which had been her father’s union slogan before it was hers. Her campaign had been polling at 11% in January. In February, at a Cebu rally, a heckler called out from the back asking what she would actually do differently. She stopped mid-sentence and answered without notes: Tatlumpung taon nating pinag-aral ang kanilang accent. Isang earnings call lang para palitan tayo. The eleven-second clip was viewed 48 million times on TikTok and Facebook within ten days. She is now polling at 27% and climbing. The crowd at Araneta is twenty-two thousand people. The heat inside the dome sits on the skin like something wet. The front three rows are Concentrix and Teleperformance retrenchers wearing their old lanyards. Outside, two hundred people who could not get tickets are watching on a mobile projector screen mounted on a flatbed truck. When the senator finishes, they do not immediately disperse. Three of them don’t leave. By the time the TV5 crew comes around, they have found red spray paint and are already halfway through her words on the flyover pillars — each letter the height of a forearm. The photograph is shared faster than the deepfake.

The five-way race has a clean logic. The two establishment candidates — a former DILG secretary backed by the Ayala and Aboitiz blocs, and a former DEPDev Secretary backed by a coalition of business chambers and the Gokongwei family — are splitting 48% of the vote between them. Neither will concede to the other. The senator needs only to hold her base. Within forty minutes of the Araneta speech, a deepfake video begins circulating on Telegram — the senator apparently endorsing a rival, voice model clean, lip-sync imperfect. COMELEC’s Task Force KKK issues a takedown request at 11:14 p.m. The platforms respond six and a half hours later. By then the clip has 3.8 million views and a second edit has been made in which the lip-sync is better.

Scene · BGC, Taguig, 13 May 2028, 11:40 p.m.

Maria Villanueva is sitting on the floor of a BGC startup office, surrounded by five annotators whose shift ended at 10, watching the COMELEC partial count on her phone. At 89% reporting, the senator is at 34.7%. The DILG secretary is at 24.1%. The DEPDev Secretary, who had been the pick of every editorial board in the country, is at 21.8%. Maria’s startup has eleven employees. Three of them voted for the senator. Three voted for the DEPDev candidate. Two voted for the DILG candidate. Three did not vote because they could not get to their home precinct. She does not ask which three.

The startup’s board meets via Zoom from Singapore, London, and San Francisco the following Monday. The meeting lasts forty-one minutes. The minutes note, in one line, that the board has resolved to “review the corporate domicile structure in light of the evolving regulatory environment.” Maria is not on the board. She reads the minutes because her boss, who is on the board, forgets to take her off the distribution list. She does not say anything. She updates her LinkedIn profile that afternoon for the first time in two years.

Three blocks from the PBCOM Tower, a crowd of about three hundred displaced BPO workers who have been watching the count outside Makati City Hall have not yet left. They do not look celebratory. They look like people waiting to see if the thing they voted for will remember they exist. The Makati Business Club issues a statement by midnight expressing concern about “policy continuity.” IBPAP issues one by 1 a.m. expressing “cautious optimism.” By morning, the Ayala Land communications team has a seventeen-point Q&A ready for media inquiries about what the election result means for commercial-property incentives. Nobody asks them about it for three days, because there is other news.

The new president is inaugurated on 30 June 2028. Her first executive order, signed at 9:14 a.m. on 1 July, establishes the “Office for Philippine Labor Sovereignty” within the Office of the President. Her second, signed the same afternoon, suspends the issuance of new GCC incentives under the CREATE MORE Act pending a “review of labor displacement impact.” The suspension lasts fourteen months and is never formally lifted — it simply stops being processed. Her third executive order, in August, requires any foreign AI model used in a Philippine commercial context to be registered with the DICT and certified “compliant with Philippine labor transition standards.” The certification process does not yet exist. The order creates the legal instrument without the administrative infrastructure to use it. This is, in the Philippines, how a policy arrives.

The measure that came closest to cushioning the displacement wave was never part of the omnibus. In September 2027, a joint Senate-House committee had converged on a draft Displaced Worker Adjustment Fund: a mandatory employer contribution requiring any company automating more than 20% of its Philippine BPO workforce in a calendar year to pay 3.5% of gross payroll into a portable, immediately accessible transition account per affected worker. The draft passed out of Senate Finance by five votes in February 2028, with bipartisan co-sponsorship from seven senators who would not normally agree on anything, including two who had been on opposite sides of every labor bill since 2018. It reached the House Ways and Means Committee in April. It died there, after an IBPAP technical working group presented an alternative “voluntary job-creation commitment” framework whose projections the committee chair found persuasive. By the time the new administration was inaugurated in June, the adjustment fund had been absorbed into the omnibus at 0.8% — where it would survive one of the two bicameral rounds before being dropped entirely. The workers who would have received portable transition accounts received, instead, the ₱50,000 one-time severance that BPO-sector HR decks described as generous.

The three competing bills — the BPO transparency levy, the expanded 4Ps, the criminal-liability clause on foreign AI — are folded into a single omnibus measure called the Philippine AI Sovereignty and Labor Protection Act, which the new administration endorses but does not actively whip. The omnibus bill passes the House by November, stalls in the Senate, and exits the year in a bicameral conference committee that has met twice. It will not clear bicam until 2030, in a form that retains the levy at 6% (down from 18%), expands 4Ps by 1.1 million households (not 1.8 million), and drops the criminal-liability clause entirely.

The oligarchs and the new administration

The Ayala Group — whose Ayala Land commercial REIT has shed 28% since 2026 as BPO office vacancies climbed past 31% in the Bay Area and Ortigas — had backed the DEPDev candidate. The post-election environment is, for Ayala, punitive without being overtly hostile: regulatory processing of their BPO-to-mixed-use rezoning applications slows from weeks to months. The SM Group, which had hedged by donating to three campaigns, gets an early meeting with the new Chief of Staff and a quiet understanding on mall-retail import duties. San Miguel Corporation, whose power-generation assets had benefited from the Iran-war fuel premium, moves fastest: by August, San Miguel Energy’s chairman has announced a ₱40-billion “Philippine AI Infrastructure Fund” in partnership with the new Office for Labor Sovereignty. What the fund will build is not specified. What San Miguel gets in return — a 25-year power-supply agreement with the proposed Bataan rehabilitation project — is specified, quietly, in a memorandum of agreement signed in September. The president, at the signing ceremony, calls the Bataan Nuclear Power Plant rehabilitation “the answer to our energy sovereignty” and forecasts first criticality by 2031. The cameras record it. The timeline will not survive its first engineering review.

The downstream effect that matters most is the one that takes longest to appear in the data. The GCC segment — the multinational-owned captive offices that had been the only growing component of the BPO sector through 2026 and 2027, adding 34,000 roles against the wave of tier-1 voice losses — begins, in the second half of 2028, to do what multinationals do when a policy environment becomes uncertain: it evaluates alternatives. Not publicly. Not with press releases. With site-selection consultants. With real-estate broker inquiries in Kuala Lumpur and Hyderabad. With informal conversations, at industry events in Singapore, about whether Manila is “stable.” By year-end, three GCCs have quietly announced regional consolidations that reduce their Philippine headcount by a combined 4,200 people. The figure does not appear in the IBPAP headline. It does not appear anywhere, except in the occupancy reports of the Ayala REIT, where it shows up as three floors going dark in Q4.

Maria Villanueva’s startup has, by November 2028, incorporated a Singapore holding company for its IP. Her ₱47,000 salary is now paid by the Philippine subsidiary of a Singaporean entity. Her equity, which had briefly been worth three times her annual salary in October, is now worth approximately four months’ salary as the valuation has been reset in a restructuring she did not vote on because she does not have board representation.

In October, when the valuation was at its high and the Series A term sheet was three weeks old, a colleague — Reina, one of the five BGC annotators, who had been watching the displacement wave with the exhausted precision of someone who had already survived one retrenchment cycle — pulled her aside during a coffee run and said plainly: Ate, i-liquidate mo na. Papel lang ‘yan. Maria had thought about it for three days. She understood paper valuation. But she was also maintaining something — performing a version of herself that had made the right bet, that had read 2028 correctly while everyone else was panicking out. She had been telling her mother, and Joseph, and herself, that things were manageable. To sell was to admit she did not believe that. She did not sell. Reina liquidated her 0.4% stake in November for ₱680,000 and sent Maria a message she did not open for two days. By January, after the Jollibee contract cancellation, Maria’s equity had compressed to ₱183,000. By February it was zero, in the restructuring. Reina never mentioned it. Maria never brought it up. The condo is current. She has taken a PAG-IBIG restructuring that extends the loan to eight years and adds ₱318,000 in total interest. Benjie’s renewal in Riyadh came through in March at the same salary but with a clause Benjie consulted the OFW legal hotline about: a performance-conditional termination right tied to comparative operational efficiency against available automated alternatives. Nanay Tess read the clause at her kitchen table in Bayambang and said: Kung gayon, anak, may plano na sila na hindi ka na lang kailangan.

By New Year’s Eve, the peso closes at ₱66.40. The BSP has spent $4.2 billion of its $108-billion reserve defending the ₱66 line and has not succeeded. The new BSP Governor — the previous one having been replaced in September after a public disagreement with the new administration over the pace of rate normalization — tells reporters that the peso’s level reflects “external factors.” He is being precise in a way that is more useful than honesty.

The senator won because she named the injury. What she was elected to do — reverse it — is not something an executive order can accomplish against a global technology transition. The administration will spend four years learning this. The economy will spend four years paying for the learning.

Chapter 04 · 2029

2029

The second shock lands in a place Manila is not watching. The Gulf domestic-labor wage curve and the humanoid RaaS curve cross in a shaded zone of employer-by-employer indifference. For Benjie Villanueva, in Riyadh, the indifference has a face, a model number, and a three-month trial clause.

Scene · Al Olaya district, Riyadh, 6 February 2029, 8:12 p.m. AST

The employer is a Saudi family named Al-Qahtani — three generations, nine people in a compound off Olaya Street, a transport and logistics business old enough to have predated the reforms. Benjie has been their driver and house-help for the last three years and seven months. He is twenty-nine now. His contract is up in eleven days. The family’s eldest son, Faisal, who runs the household side of the business, has called Benjie into the majlis to talk. The room is air-conditioned to the point of chill, and Benjie is sweating anyway.

Faisal is apologetic. He is specific. The QSS × Humanoid joint venture — a Saudi sovereign-fund subsidiary that had announced a 10,000-unit domestic deployment in November 2025 — had finally crossed, in Q4 2028, into the affordable mass-market tier. The units were being sold as a managed service at SAR 1,400 per month, all-in — roughly $373 — with a three-month performance guarantee. Faisal had taken a pilot unit in October. It could drive in city conditions after a four-week geofenced training period. It could iron a thobe. In November it lost GPS signal in the underground garage and scraped the side of the Land Cruiser; the repair cost had been deducted from the vendor’s performance guarantee, not from the Al-Qahtani household budget. In December it needed a firmware update that locked it out of operation for eleven hours and required a support engineer from the vendor’s Olaya office. Faisal had called Benjie that morning. Benjie had come and completed the school run. These incidents had been logged, resolved, and noted. None of them had changed the arithmetic. The unit could not, yet, be trusted with the children on its own, but Faisal’s wife did not leave the children alone with Benjie either, so this was not a regression. The unit did not need to be housed, fed, or sent home for Eid. It did not have a mother in Pangasinan who was, every February, a little sicker than she had been last February.

Faisal is still speaking. He is offering Benjie a supervisory role — overseeing three of the units as they deployed across a broader family compound network, at a salary of SAR 3,200 per month. This is roughly 45% of Benjie’s current package. Benjie is doing the arithmetic in his head. ₱28,000 a month to Nanay Tess becomes ₱13,000. Sophia’s tuition at Miriam Elementary comes from that money. Maria’s condo restructuring assumed Benjie’s remittance at current levels. He says, in the way OFWs have learned to say difficult things to Gulf employers: Thank you, sir, for the consideration. May I have some time to discuss with my family?

Faisal nods. He says: Of course, Benjamin. But please understand, I cannot hold the supervisory role open past next week. Three other drivers in our network are also transitioning.

The aggregate pattern, visible only in retrospect, is this. Of the 720,000 Filipino workers in Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Qatar, Kuwait, Bahrain, and Oman at the start of 2029, roughly 174,000 receive non-renewal notices during the year. Another 91,000 receive renewal offers at 40–55% of their previous compensation, attached to supervisory roles for which most of them have not been trained. About 38,000 accept the reduced roles. The remainder return. DMW — the Department of Migrant Workers, which absorbed POEA’s overseas-deployment functions in 2022 — processes 162,000 repatriations between March and December, more than triple the 2025 figure, and runs out of budgeted support per returnee by mid-September.

NAIA Terminal 1, on a Sunday in April, handles 4,100 Gulf returnees in a single day — more than its design throughput. The OWWA welcome desk, staffed for 200, runs out of printed information packets by 10 a.m. Families waiting in the arrival concourse hold cardboard signs. Some of them are welcome signs. Some are not. One reads, in red marker: OWWA NASAAN KAYO. The photograph is shared 2.1 million times. OWWA’s spokesperson issues a statement using the phrase “actively monitored” four times in three paragraphs. Outside the arrival gate, three DMW officers are processing documents at a folding table because the office ran out of counter space in February and the renovation budget was frozen. The folding table is still there in December.

Remittances from the Gulf corridors fall 19% year-on-year in 2029, the sharpest single-year decline since 1997. The US corridor holds, because the US corridor is disproportionately professional-class and healthcare workers whose roles have not yet crossed the humanoid threshold. But the Gulf corridor is 23% of the remittance base, and its decline is not, yet, offset anywhere. Total personal remittances for 2029 print at $34.8 billion, against the 2028 figure of $39.1 billion. The ratio to GDP falls to 5.9%, against 2025’s 7.3%.

Scene · Bayambang, Pangasinan, 14 March 2029, 11:45 a.m.

Nanay Tess is at the kitchen table, where she conducts all of the family’s difficult conversations. Benjie is on a video call from his shared flat in Riyadh. Maria is on the same call from Mandaluyong, with Sophia in the background asking why lola is crying. Nanay Tess is not crying. She has already done the crying, alone, the night before. She is doing the arithmetic now.

The family’s decision, after forty-one minutes of discussion, is that Benjie will take the supervisory role for twelve months — long enough to accumulate return capital, bank a small reserve against the peso, and see whether the new administration’s “labor sovereignty” framework produces anything that might protect him. He will not renew past March 2030. Nanay Tess does not say, but all three of them understand, that she will sell the three-hectare family riceland to Maria’s cousin Rommel for a price that is 31% below the 2023 valuation and 19% above the 2030 valuation she does not yet know is coming. The sale closes in June. By then the administration’s Office for Philippine Labor Sovereignty has issued seventeen press releases and zero enforceable protections for workers in Benjie’s situation.

The cascade begins

In an economy whose peso has no second stabilizer — where the BPO corridor and the remittance corridor are the two pillars of the current account — a simultaneous impairment of both is not additive. It is compounding. The BSP has been defending ₱66 since mid-2028. It cannot defend ₱66, ₱70, and ₱74 in sequence against two failing legs. By the end of 2029, the peso closes at ₱71.20. The Monetary Board, in December, raises rates 75 basis points. The Governor uses the word unprecedented at the press conference. He is not exaggerating.

At the same time, in other corridors, other clocks are ticking. Hong Kong and Singapore, which had explicitly exempted foreign domestic workers from their 2025 work-permit reforms, begin quiet consultations in Q3 2029 on permit-cap reductions starting 2031. Japan’s 370,000-caregiver shortage prompts METI and MHLW to fund a humanoid-caregiving pilot in Nagoya. The Yara Birkeland’s autonomous-mooring upgrade, after six years of delay, enters sea trials. None of these is, in 2029, a shock. Each of them, in 2029, is a headline in the Nikkei or the Lloyd’s List that Filipino officials do not read carefully.

Maria Villanueva, in Mandaluyong, is thirty-two years old. The startup she works for raises a Series A in October at a valuation that briefly, improbably, makes her vested equity worth three times her annual salary on paper. The following month, the firm’s largest customer — a Jollibee-affiliated food-service integrator — cancels its contract, because the US frontier lab it had been piloting against had reached parity on Taglish. The valuation compresses 62% within eleven weeks. Maria’s paper equity is, by Christmas, worth approximately three months’ salary. By this point, she is past being surprised.

Three of the five annotators who had been in her BGC office have, by the end of the year, taken work through a platform called CognitaWork — a Singapore-registered, Chinese-capital-backed human-in-the-loop service that uses displaced Philippine workers as QA reviewers for a mainland AI training pipeline. The pay is ₱12,000 a month, in digital-wallet credits, for eight hours a day of reviewing synthetic-media clips for factual accuracy and detecting jailbreak attempts in model outputs. The work is technically legal. It is entirely unregulated. It does not appear in any IBPAP report. It does not appear in the PSA labor survey. One of the three quit after two weeks because the content moderation queue — unsorted, unfiltered, paid by the clip — was, in her words, not something she was willing to do for any amount of money. The other two are still there in December. They do not discuss it in the group chat.

Scene · Cubao, Quezon City, 31 December 2029, 11:52 p.m.

The fireworks start before midnight, the way they always do. The Araneta Coliseum rooftop across the skyline catches the first burst — someone in Tondo with deep pockets, or maybe Marikina, the plume too broad to locate. By 11:57 the sky above EDSA is unreadable: green and gold and the sharp sulfur smell of illegal pyrotechnics drifting south. Maria watches from the fire escape landing outside her unit. The concrete is warm under her bare feet. Elijah is asleep inside. The peso had closed that afternoon at ₱71.20. She knows this the way she knows the weather — not as a policy variable, as a physical condition of the air. The decade that is ending had taken her Concentrix headset, her startup equity, and her brother’s remittance, in that order. The decade beginning — 2030, the one everyone had spent twenty years saying — is forty-five seconds away. The sky is very loud. She stays until the smoke makes her eyes water, then goes back inside and locks the sliding door, and the noise becomes something that is happening to someone else.

The cascade was never going to be a single moment. It was always going to be a sequence of Tuesdays, across several countries, in which specific families discovered that their arithmetic no longer closed.

Chapter 05 · 2030

2030

The peso breaks. BPO and remittances fail together. Six corners of the economy, in the same week of March, discover they are running on the same broken engine.

The week of 11–17 March 2030 · six views

On Monday morning, 11 March 2030, the peso opens at ₱74.80 and crosses ₱76 before lunch. The Singapore NDF market had already been pricing ₱77 since the previous Friday; onshore is catching up. The BSP leans against the disorderly move at ₱76.40 with $1.1 billion, smoothing the descent for ninety-seven minutes. By 3 p.m., the market trades through anyway — the rate is at ₱77.20 and the intervention has cost $1.9 billion of the $94-billion reserve. At 4 p.m., the Monetary Board convenes an emergency meeting. At 6:40 p.m., the Governor announces a 125-basis-point hike — the sharpest single move since the Asian Financial Crisis. At 7:15 p.m., a sell-side strategist at a Tokyo-based house publishes the note that will, within eleven days, be followed by a ratings-watch-negative — the first step toward the sovereign-credit downgrade of the decade.

On the street, the Monday reads differently. Money changers along Kamuning Road lower their shutters to half-mast by noon and begin quoting ₱2 above the interbank rate — the informal signal that they expect the rate to be worse by closing. The BDO ATM on Shaw dispenses nothing after noon. GCash shows “transaction limit reached” on transfers above ₱5,000. A 7-Eleven on Boni Avenue tapes a square of cardboard to the register: CASH ONLY / POS OFFLINE. Three SM Supermall branches in Metro Manila post per-customer limits on cooking oil, canned goods, and rice by early afternoon. The SM North Edsa branch calls for PNP backup at the checkout by 3 p.m. The request is logged. It is actioned two hours and forty minutes later, because three PNP mobile units are committed to the Malacañang perimeter detail and the remaining patrol vehicles in the district are running on fuel rations that have limited non-priority responses since February. By then, the checkout has resolved itself. Nobody is seriously hurt. Seventeen traffic incidents are logged by MMDA on EDSA between noon and 5 p.m. Two are not minor.

Black Tuesday

The PSEi opens Tuesday morning at 6,038, down 3.9% on the Monday close. The exchange has not yet processed the implications of the rate decision.

By 10:30 a.m. the index is at 5,882. Down 6.4%. At 11:03 a.m., it crosses the 10% threshold — the first Level 1 circuit-breaker halt since March 2020. Fifteen minutes of silence. The screens go gray. The floor empties into the corridor and stands there, not speaking, because there is nothing to say that the number has not already said.

The halt lifts at 11:18. Selling resumes immediately. By 11:40, the index has gapped to −14.6% before a partial recovery pulls it back. Philippine ten-year treasury yields gap 210 basis points wider in the secondary market. The Bureau of the Treasury defers the scheduled Thursday T-bond auction. Five-year sovereign CDS spreads blow past 380 basis points — the widest since 2009.

BDO Unibank: −13.1%. Ayala Land, whose analyst coverage has been flagging BPO-office-vacancy exposure for eighteen months: −15.4%. SM Prime Holdings, whose anchor-tenant restructurings the previous quarter had been reported under “portfolio resilience”: −11.2%.

The PSE closes the session at 5,891. Down 11.8%. A loss of ₱1.4 trillion in market capitalization in a single day — the worst session since the 2008 Global Financial Crisis.

By 3 p.m. Manila time, every sell-side note out of Singapore, Hong Kong, and Tokyo has used the same two words. By 5 p.m., they are the Inquirer’s push notification. By 7 p.m., they are a trending sound on TikTok Philippines, set to footage of a trading floor screen with the numbers in red. The day has a name now. It will not lose it.

i. Ortigas · Tuesday

The law offices of the DLA Piper-affiliate firm handling the dissolution of the Taglish voice-AI startup Maria Villanueva works for are on the 34th floor of a building whose BPO tenants vacated eighteen months ago. The partner explains the liquidation waterfall in thirty-one minutes. Equity holders — Maria included — recover nothing. Her final paycheck, prorated to 17 March, is ₱26,400. She withdraws it at the BPI ATM on Ortigas Avenue in two transactions, because the daily limit has been reduced to ₱15,000 and the machine will not dispense more. The condo payment is due on the 30th. The PAG-IBIG restructuring is now in arrears. She calls Joseph, her old Concentrix supervisor, who is running a trade-school franchise in Marikina. He offers her a customer-support job at ₱24,000 a month. She takes it before he has finished the offer.

ii. Bayambang · Wednesday

Nanay Tess cannot afford the Ventolin inhaler Sophia had been using since the previous rainy season. The pharmacy in Dagupan has it at ₱1,840, up from ₱1,120 at the start of the year. The generic substitute is out of stock at three pharmacies in sequence. Rice at the wet market is ₱78 a kilo, up from ₱54 in January. Nanay Tess converts the last of her teacher’s-pension dollar savings — $680, held in a BPI savings account since 2018 — at ₱77.40. iii. National Food Authority, Quezon City · Thursday

The rice-import tender for Q2 2030 closes on Thursday. The National Food Authority, re-authorized by Executive Order 112 in January 2030 to conduct commercial tenders after the private-sector import framework under the 2019 Rice Tariffication Law failed to mobilize sufficient volume at the peso’s crisis-year exchange rate, had budgeted for 1.2 million metric tonnes at an average $540 per tonne, financed in dollars. The tender clears at $612. The NFA funds the difference, in pesos, at a rate that has moved 11% against budget in seven weeks. The Vietnamese supplier, asked informally by a DA undersecretary whether volume could be expanded, replies that the Vietnamese domestic harvest is being prioritized for a recently-signed bilateral arrangement with Saudi Arabia. The Philippine rice self-sufficiency print for 2030, released in December, will come in at 62.1% — the lowest figure in the seventy-year history of the measurement.

iv. Calamba · Friday

The Samsung Electro-Mechanics plant in Calamba, which had opened in 2026 with a PHP 50.65-billion commitment and a 3,200-head payroll, announces a 22% reduction in force and a pivot of the remaining automotive-power-IC line to Penang. The official reason is “strategic rebalancing.” The unofficial reason, discussed in a Korean-language briefing a week earlier, is that the Malaysian ringgit had held against the dollar while the peso had not, that Penang’s power cost per kWh is a third of Calamba’s, and that the Philippine political risk premium had, in the two years since the 2028 election, priced in a populist presidency. DTI issues a press release calling the move “temporary.” Nobody believes the press release.

v. Luzon grid · Friday, 8:40 p.m.

A yellow alert on the Luzon grid, the eleventh of 2030, escalates to red at 8:40 p.m. Demand is 12,140 MW against available supply of 11,430 MW. Manual load-shedding rolls through Metro Manila in thirty-minute windows. Maria’s district in Mandaluyong loses power from 9:30 to 10:00. The heat arrives within four minutes — the concrete holds it, and without the window unit there is nowhere for it to go. The fridge, by the time the power comes back, has begun to sweat. Elijah, now eleven, is doing his Grade 6 homework by the flashlight on her phone. He asks, quietly, why the lights keep going out this year. Maria tells him: because the country cannot afford its own electricity. She does not mean this as a political statement. She means it in the way one states a fact of physics.

vi. Malacañang · Sunday

A protest has been camped outside the Palace since Thursday. The PNP estimates 4,000. The organizers claim 14,000. The perimeter fence on J. P. Laurel Street has been reinforced with two rows of water-cannon trucks since Friday. On Saturday afternoon, a DSWD undersecretary whose car is briefly surrounded at the E. Quintos gate describes the experience to a colleague as “genuinely frightening.” The colleague — who has been on continuous peso-monitoring duty since Monday and has slept in the office four nights — does not find this surprising.

On Sunday evening, the President addresses the nation for nineteen minutes. The address has been drafted and redrafted seven times over the preceding eighty hours. The final version does not use the words recession, crisis, or emergency. It uses headwinds four times, resilience six times, and sovereignty once, toward the end. It announces a PHP 180-billion supplementary budget, to be funded through a combination of ADB emergency lending, a one-time levy on digital-services firms, and what the Secretary of Finance later describes as optimizing existing allocations. The DPWH 2030 capex is cut by PHP 64 billion to make room. The 4Ps expansion — which had cleared the House in the 2028 omnibus bill and spent two years in Senate bicam — is formally deferred to 2032.

The ADB facility had been announced in a press statement the previous Friday, before the address. A senator from the majority coalition — who had been the Luzon operations lead of the 2028 campaign — is reached by phone by a GMA News reporter that Sunday evening. He is asked whether accepting ADB conditionality is consistent with the administration’s sovereign-labor mandate. Ang paghahanap ng tulong ay hindi pagsusuko ng kalayaan, he says. The reporter asks whether this means yes or no. The senator hangs up.

Why this one doesn’t recover the way the others did

The Philippines has lived through four modern crises. In 1983–85, the Marcos debt crisis resolved when the politics resolved — Aquino flew home, the dictatorship fell, the IMF arrived, and the structural repair began. In 1997–98, the peso collapsed because the Thai baht had collapsed first; the region recovered together, and BPO investment arrived during the hangover as a structural dividend. In 2008–09, the global crisis barely touched the Philippines: BPO revenues kept growing, remittances held, and the country grew 1.1% when its neighbors contracted. In 2020, the pandemic was an external shock with a defined endpoint — vaccines, reopening, and a V-shaped recovery financed by remittances that rose, counterintuitively, as OFWs stopped spending abroad and wired home instead.

Each of those crises had a recovery mechanism. A political reset. A regional co-recovery. A growth sector absorbing the displaced. An external stimulus that arrived on a knowable schedule. This crisis has none of those. The BPO jobs do not come back when conditions improve, because cheaper and more capable AI is the improvement — it is a permanent cost reduction for the buyers of labor, not a cyclical one. The Gulf remittance corridor does not reopen when the Philippine government negotiates better bilateral terms, because the humanoid RaaS price keeps falling regardless of bilateral terms. And for the first time in fifty years, the two pillars that absorbed every previous crisis — BPO and OFW — are failing simultaneously, in the same half-decade. There is no third pillar. The country is attempting to run a ₱180-billion supplementary budget from a fiscal position whose tax effort is 14% of GDP — the lowest in ASEAN — against a current account that has lost its two structural anchors at once. This is not a cycle. It is a ratchet.

The underlying capability curve

Prediction-market aggregates tracked on Metaculus as of mid-2026 place AGI-level capability — systems matching or exceeding human performance across most economically-valuable cognitive tasks — at approximately 40% probability by 2030 and 65% by 2033. The direction of this curve is not contested. The specific numbers are. What is not contested: the systems that triggered the 2026–2027 BPO wave are, in 2030, materially more capable, not less. The Gulf humanoid crossover of 2029 is an early-tranche event in a multi-year cascade. Nothing has stopped. This is the context in which the Philippine state is attempting to run a supplementary budget.

vii. Metrobank Credit Risk, Makati · The following Monday

On Monday morning, a senior credit risk officer at Metropolitan Bank & Trust processes a covenant-compliance sweep for the commercial real estate portfolio. The sweep is routine. It is run quarterly. It has not, in the four years he has been running it, produced what it produces now: two developer borrowers — one Megaworld entity holding the Eastwood Cyberpark portfolio, one Robinsons Land subsidiary in Ortigas — whose debt-service coverage ratios have fallen below 1.10×. The covenant threshold is standard. Crossing it does not, in itself, mean default. It means the bank must either grant a formal waiver or declare a technical breach, which triggers cross-default clauses in the borrowers’ parallel facilities at BDO and BPI.

He calls his supervisor. His supervisor calls the head of corporate lending. The conversation runs forty-five minutes and produces the following decision: seek a waiver letter from both borrowers before issuing any formal event-of-default notice; coordinate informally with the syndicate banks before any disclosure. The head of corporate lending uses the phrase market-sensitive information twice. The credit risk officer does not ask what that means, because he knows what it means. It means that if Metrobank triggers cross-default before BDO and BPI have had the same internal conversation, Metrobank moves first in a sequence that nobody wants to start. So Metrobank requests the waiver. BDO, by separate internal process, reaches the same posture by 2 p.m. BPI reaches it by Thursday. The waivers are granted — pro forma, temporary, and without prejudice — within seven days. The covenant breaches are, in the Q1 2030 disclosures, described as “under active monitoring.”

What the covenant sweep does not capture — because it is designed to capture collateral risk, not consumer credit risk — is the second wave assembling in the retail portfolio. BSP’s pandemic-era restructuring regime, extended through 2029, had allowed banks to carry displaced-worker personal loans as “performing” as long as any payment had been received within the restructuring window. The 12–18 month re-default cycle on those portfolios begins in mid-2030. By December, the NPL ratio that the three universal banks report at 3.4% is carrying a shadow cohort — restructured BPO-household loans one missed payment from reclassification — that Metrobank’s internal stress models estimate at a further 2.1 percentage points. The models are not published. They are not required to be.

The Monetary Board’s 125-basis-point hike from the previous Monday is, at the variable-rate end of the retail book, already repricing exactly the household loans closest to re-default. The BSP Governor knew this when he signed the decision. The hike happened anyway, because not defending the peso was worse — the least-bad decision in a set that contained no good ones. The credit risk officer closes his laptop at 6:20 p.m. The covenant-breach report sits in his drafts folder, unsent, where it will remain for thirty-one days until a compliance review forces the question. He takes the elevator from the 18th floor. Fourteen of the floors below him are occupied by tenants whose debt-service coverage he has not yet been asked to check.

By December 2030, the cumulative direct BPO displacement since end-2025 is 752,000 jobs, inside the research dossier’s 2026 projection band of 750K–950K by 2030 under the severe scenario. Informal-sector second-order losses, estimated by IBON, run roughly 600,000 additional. Total formal-sector unemployment, by the PSA’s narrow measure, stands at 7.8%. The PSA’s labor-force participation rate falls to 60.4%, the lowest since 2011. Neither of them is, in any politically consequential way, addressed.

The peso closes the year at ₱79.60. The BSP’s reserve has fallen from $108 billion to $79 billion across twelve months. The sovereign-credit downgrade of March has been followed by two further rating actions. Philippine dollar bonds trade at BB+ levels at the short end and BB at the long. Capital controls have not been formally discussed in public. They have been discussed in private, by the people who would discuss them, in a meeting room in the BSP complex in Malate, twice.

The devaluation produces one unmistakable beneficiary. San Miguel Corporation’s peso-denominated power-generation assets — priced in pesos, denominated against dollar-linked electricity benchmarks — expand their operating margin for the third consecutive quarter. SM Prime Holdings, whose malls had hemorrhaged BPO anchor tenants since 2027, renegotiates four major anchor leases at 55–60% of prior rates and books the restructurings under “portfolio resilience.” Ayala Land, which had filed for BPO-to-residential rezoning on three BGC parcels in March, receives its first approval in December: fourteen months after the application, twelve months after the election. The approval is signed by an official who had been a Makati City Hall career employee until August 2028, when a new administration meant new appointments. No one remarks on the timing, because remarking on the timing is not how Manila works.

2031

The reckoning arrives in the form of a term sheet. The safety net is tested. It does not hold. The political response to its failure reshapes the state.

Scene · DOF boardroom, Roxas Boulevard, 14 February 2031, 9:00 a.m.

The meeting is nominally a coordination session on the 2032 national expenditure program. The attendees are the Secretary of Finance, the Secretary of DEPDev, the Secretary of Budget and Management, the BSP Governor, the DSWD Secretary, and two senior economists from an IMF Article IV mission who had arrived in Manila four weeks earlier and had not, technically, been invited. The cabinet officials in the room are all the president’s appointees — three of them former labor-rights advocates from the same Mindanao advocacy network that produced her candidacy, one a longtime party operative, one a technocrat accelerated into the BSP governorship after the previous governor’s replacement in September 2028. None of them had been IMF-cooperation partisans. None of them had expected to become IMF-cooperation partisans. The IMF economists do not speak for the first forty minutes. When they speak, they use the phrase extended fund facility three times in twelve seconds.

The arithmetic on the table is brutal and bounded. The 4Ps expansion — 4.4 million to 6.2 million households — costs PHP 74 billion incremental annually. The displacement supplement for BPO-layoff households costs a further PHP 48 billion. The expanded SSS unemployment insurance, if made six-month recurring, costs PHP 62 billion. Total new social-protection spend at the minimum politically viable level: PHP 184 billion. Available fiscal space, after debt service and mandated operating expenditures at the 2031 revenue forecast: PHP 41 billion. The DPWH 2031 capex, already cut once in 2030, stands at PHP 762 billion. The Secretary of Finance proposes a further 18% cut.

The DSWD Secretary, whose agency would run the expanded transfer program, takes four minutes to argue the political impossibility of the cut. The DEPDev Secretary, whose agency had spent two years modeling the infrastructure multiplier, argues the economic impossibility. The BSP Governor, who had defended the peso at ₱76 and ₱79 and was now defending it at ₱82, argues that no combination of cuts and transfers fixes the underlying current-account problem without an external program. The senior IMF economist, very quietly, agrees.

The fiscal trilemma, 2031

The state cannot simultaneously (a) expand the safety net to catch the displacement cohort, (b) maintain infrastructure capex that sustains the non-BPO growth base, and (c) service debt at the sovereign-downgrade spread. Two of the three have to give. The political incentive in an election year is to protect the first, cut the second, and externalize the third through IMF terms.

The extended fund facility arrangement is announced on 11 March — exactly one year after the peso break of the previous spring. The terms are, by any historical IMF standard, light: fiscal-consolidation path to 4.0% deficit by 2033, digital-services-tax expansion, rationalization of tax incentives that AMRO’s 2025 ASEAN+3 bilateral surveillance report had flagged, and a monitoring framework on BSP FX intervention. The terms are, by Philippine political standards, humiliating. The President’s approval rating, which had entered the year at 34%, falls to 21% in the April Pulse Asia. By June, the word Estrada is being used in the op-eds, by people old enough to remember what Estrada means in this context.

On the day of the announcement, protests erupt in six cities simultaneously — Manila, Cebu, Davao, Cagayan de Oro, Iloilo, and Zamboanga. The Manila rally at Liwasang Bonifacio draws an estimated 28,000 people by mid-afternoon. The Cebu rally turns violent when a water cannon is deployed at 4 p.m.; two protesters are hospitalized with non-life-threatening injuries. The organizers — three of whom had been on the 2028 campaign steering committee, one of whom had written the labor-sovereignty platform plank — issue a statement that does not mention the IMF by name and instead uses the phrase economic surrender. Within forty-eight hours, the phrase appears in spray paint on eleven government building walls in Metro Manila. The Presidential Communications Office issues a statement condemning vandalism. It does not address the substance.

The geopolitical auction

Within seventy-two hours of the IMF announcement, the Chinese Embassy conveys, through back channels to the DOF, a ₱380-billion infrastructure financing offer: 3.2% fixed, forty-year tenor, no fiscal-consolidation conditions, and no mention of the three EDCA sites commissioned in Cagayan and Batanes since 2023. The offer does not need to mention them. The US Embassy, for its part, conveys its support for the IMF program and schedules a joint military exercise in the West Philippine Sea for the following month — the largest since 2029. Neither government makes a public statement that could be described as pressure. Both governments are applying pressure. The Philippine Cabinet, which must decide whether to accept the Chinese offer before the IMF board meeting in April, does not reach consensus. The offer is neither accepted nor declined. It is, in the language of the DOF minutes, “under active review.” It remains under active review for fourteen months.

The safety net tests fail in sequence. PhilHealth, whose fraud crisis of 2024 had never fully resolved, discovers in May that its auto-renewal for 2.1 million displaced BPO workers had lapsed without notification; emergency legislation restores coverage but not confidence. SSS unemployment benefits, paid at the one-time legacy formula, reach 340,000 claimants in 2031 against a displaced pool of more than a million. The 4Ps expansion, deferred in 2030, is deferred again to 2033. TESDA’s emergency reskilling program, funded at PHP 8 billion in 2028, has spent PHP 4.2 billion and graduated 31,000 workers into a labor market whose relevant job postings, in 2031, are down 47% year-on-year.

Scene · Mandaluyong, 22 August 2031, 6:50 p.m.

Maria is thirty-four. She has been, for eleven months, a supervisor at the trade-school franchise Joseph had offered her at ₱24,000 a month. The franchise now pays her ₱28,000, which in 2031 pesos buys roughly what ₱19,000 had bought at the start of 2029. Elijah is in Grade 7 at a public school because the Miriam Elementary tuition had become mathematically impossible in June. Sophia is in Grade 3 at the same public school. The condo, after two further restructurings, is twenty-four months from freehold. Maria has not missed a payment, which she counts as her single non-negotiable.

Benjie came home in March 2030 and has not been able to leave since. DMW deployment of new domestic-worker contracts to the Gulf has fallen 74% against 2025. He drives for Grab in Antipolo. The Grab rate per ride, in real terms, has fallen 22% since 2028 because the rider pool has tripled. He remits nothing, because there is nothing to remit. He lives with Nanay Tess in Pangasinan on weekdays and in Mandaluyong on weekends, sleeping on the sofa in Maria’s living room. This is, for the Villanuevas, the new equilibrium. Nanay Tess, at sixty-three, has stopped mentioning the chest tightness she had brought up in every phone call since the wet season. Maria does not ask whether this means it has passed or whether her mother has decided there is no point in saying it.

On the evening of 22 August, Maria and Benjie sit on the floor of the condo with a printed-out spreadsheet between them. The spreadsheet is Maria’s budget for September. It is off by ₱7,400. Benjie looks at the number and says: Ate, we can try selling the condo. Maria says: Hindi naman puwede, walang bumibili. Tatlong unit sa aming palapag ang 'for sale' since January — wala pang takers. Benjie looks at the spreadsheet. He does not look at her. Ate, pasensya na. Wala talaga akong nagawa. His voice does not break. It does something worse — it goes flat, in the way voices go flat when they have practiced the sentence too many times before saying it aloud. Maria puts her hand on his arm. She holds it for a moment, then lets go, because if she holds it any longer she will not be able to stop what comes after. The condo has equity on paper. The market has no buyers to convert it to cash. The electricity, which has been on rolling brownouts for eight months, goes out at 7:04.

In September, Reina — the annotator who had told Maria to liquidate, the one who had been right — sends a message to the old BGC group chat. She has been approved for the Canadian Express Entry skilled-worker stream. Her CRS score was 471, boosted by English proficiency and three years of verifiable tech-adjacent work. She is leaving in November. Her sister, who had been a PhilHealth claims processor until the 2031 auto-renewal lapse eliminated her unit, is applying for the same stream. The message is three sentences long. Nobody replies with congratulations. Two people reply with questions about the application process. One asks whether the English test is the same IELTS they used to proctor for the call centers. It is.

Reina is not unusual. The Canadian Express Entry draws for 2031 include 14,200 Filipino principal applicants — the highest single-country total after India. Australia’s Subclass 482 temporary-skill-shortage visa processes 11,400. The UAE’s Golden Visa, expanded in 2030, takes another 4,600 on tech-adjacent credentials. The people leaving are not the poorest. They are the ones with enough credential, enough English, enough documentation to qualify — the exact cohort the BPO sector had built over twenty years. The country is exporting the asset it can least afford to lose, and calling it labor mobility.

Three weeks later, a contact from the trade-school network passes Maria a referral for a gray-market QA role: reviewing AI training data for a Singapore-registered platform, ₱15,000 a month in digital-wallet credits, the same class of work three of her former BGC colleagues had taken in 2029. She takes a week with it. She is not squeamish about gray-market work in principle — she has been managing gray-market supply-chain documentation for Joseph’s franchise for eight months. She turns the referral down for a different reason, one she does not fully articulate even to Benjie: she will not spend the next eighteen months making someone else’s model more accurate at the tasks that cost her everything. She enrolls in the TESDA Transition Corps instead: a digital-operations track with ADB co-financing, 47,000 enrollment slots, a reported placement rate of 71%, and three active GCC placement partnerships. When she starts in September 2031, the program is, genuinely, working.

It has been working since July 2030. Completion rates have held at 71%. Three independent evaluations, including one by DEPDev’s monitoring office, have called it cost-effective at PHP 84,000 per verified placement — well within international benchmarks for active labor programs. Then the 2032 budget reconciliation arrives. The IMF program’s fiscal-consolidation path requires the Philippine government to provide a PHP 3.8 billion match to unlock the next ADB tranche. The match is diverted to debt service. The ADB tranche suspends in November 2031. The three GCC placement partners, which have been conducting their own Kuala Lumpur site-selection reviews in parallel with their Philippine placement commitments, let their partnership agreements quietly lapse. The program is not cancelled by decree. It is not defunded in a press release. It is simply not matched. Active enrollment falls from 47,000 to 9,000 by March 2032. Maria completes her coursework in February 2032. The placement office informs her that the GCC partnerships are “under review.” They remain under review. The program that was working enters the 2032 safety-net inventory alongside the PhilHealth lapses and the deferred 4Ps expansion, because by 2032 it has become indistinguishable from them.

A safety net that was not designed for a shock of this character cannot hold a shock of this character. It was built as a targeting instrument, not a stabilizer. The failure is not a surprise. The surprise is that it was not rebuilt in the three years between the warning and the event.
Chapter 06 · The reckoning

The political tail of 2031 is the midterm election held on the second Monday of May, and its verdict is not ambiguous. The president’s coalition — the Sovereign Labor Alliance and allied slates — loses fourteen House seats and three Senate positions. The Senate, which had been narrowly aligned with the administration through 2030, moves into de facto opposition control. Exit polling released that week by Pulse Asia names the economy, rice prices, and the IMF program, in that order, as the top three reasons voters chose opposition candidates. The word sovereignty does not appear in the exit data.

The president acknowledges the result in a televised address. She calls it “a mandate for more urgency.” Her own coalition senators, in private, use different language. The administration will govern for three more years — until the 2034 presidential election ends her term — with a budget it cannot pass and a legislature it cannot manage. The displaced cohort that had voted for her in 2028 on the promise of labor sovereignty has, in 2031, voted for whoever was not her coalition. She has delivered the IMF program she promised not to need, and lost the chambers she needed to deliver anything else. The 4Ps expansion, deferred from 2030, is now legislatively dead for the remainder of her term.

A reformist opposition senator — a former NEDA economist who runs on a platform of honest reckoning and IMF cooperation without euphemism — uses a phrase in a panel interview on ANC in December that will be quoted through the rest of the decade: We are not arguing about which policy to pursue. We are arguing about which disaster to admit to. The country that admits to the right disaster still has a decade ahead of it. The country that admits to the wrong one does not. She begins, that same month, preparing a 2034 presidential run. She is polling at 38%. The question for 2034 is whether there will be anything left to reckon with.

Chapter 07 · 2032

2032

What did not come back. Six years in, the shocks have stopped arriving because there is nothing left that the next shock can break. This is the floor. The Philippines, in October 2032, is a smaller country than it was in 2025 by every measure that the planners of 2025 had tracked.

Scene · Mandaluyong, Tuesday, 19 October 2032, 4:48 p.m.

Maria Villanueva is thirty-five years old. She is standing at the sari-sari store on the ground floor of a building that, five years ago, she had believed she would own outright by 2030. The building has not had reliable elevator service since March. The notice from the HOA, which she reads every week out of a habit that has curdled into superstition, announces that a new maintenance vendor will begin service on 1 November. The previous three vendors had also begun service on a 1 November, in different years, under different names. The elevators were last reliably serviced in 2028. She carries the groceries up twenty-three flights. By the fourteenth floor the plastic bag handles have cut white lines into her fingers.

She pays ₱105 for a kilo of rice — the premium import, because the NFA subsidy queue in Shaw had run out by 10 a.m. — and a week of candles. Behind the counter, a cardboard sign taped to the glass since March reads: NO UTANG MUNA / PASENSYA NA PO. Underneath, in someone else’s handwriting: lahat tayo ganito. The peso closes at ₱81.40. Sophia’s Ventolin, which Nanay Tess had not been able to get in the wet pharmacy in Dagupan in March 2030, is again stocked at the Mercury Drug across the street. It costs ₱2,140 per inhaler, against ₱1,120 in January 2025. Maria buys it once every forty-six days. Insurance covers none of it because PhilHealth, which had lapsed her family’s auto-renewal twice in 2031, still shows her account status as “under reconciliation” after fourteen months of filings.

Sophia is ten. Elijah is thirteen. Elijah has not been to a birthday party in a classmate’s home since Grade 6, because the classmates whose homes he used to visit have moved to Cavite or Pampanga or Canada. The Miriam Elementary class of 2030, Sophia’s original cohort, has lost 43% of its enrollment to the public system. Sophia does not know what Miriam Elementary was. Maria does not tell her. There is no way to tell her that is not cruel.

The Philippines in October 2032 is not recovering. It is, instead, at the bottom of what one BSP research note — released in April 2032, read by no one outside a thirty-person distribution list — calls the stable trough. The note argues, correctly, that the economy has stopped contracting because the contractionable sectors have contracted. The BPO sector sits at 1.02 million direct workers, against 1.90 million at the start of 2026. Services exports, after three consecutive years of decline, grew 1.1% in 2031 — an artifact of denominator collapse, not of new demand. The current-account deficit has stabilized at 5.4% of GDP, financed by concessional rollovers from ADB, JICA, and the continuing IMF arrangement. The peso has stabilized at ₱81 because the BSP has, after three years of failed defense, accepted the level.

Stabilization is not recovery. It is, in 2032, the term the technocracy uses because the political system cannot use the accurate term, which is surrender.

The street-level register for this is not dramatic. It is a shorter queue at the Jollibee. It is the sari-sari store owner who has stopped extending credit — not out of cruelty, but because she has extended it to everyone and it amounts to the same thing. It is a barangay tanod in a monobloc chair at the subdivision gate at 11 p.m. because the HOA stopped paying the private security company in June and the barangay sent someone instead. It is people who are still formally employed, by the PSA’s measure, who have stopped telling other people what they do. Below the formal economy, the informal one has done what it always does: absorbed the shock, distributed it, and kept people alive. The sari-sari credit networks, the paluwagan savings circles, Rommel’s rice cooperative in Pangasinan, Joseph’s trade-school franchise, the Grab pool that tripled in eighteen months — these are not nothing. They are the reason the trough is a trough and not a collapse. They are also not recovery. They are survival priced at 40% of what the same people earned in 2025, in structures that offer no insurance, no upward path, and no political voice. The unrest of 2029, 2030, and 2031 — the Liwasang rallies, the spray-painted walls, the airport signs — did not end. It became weather.

The slide does not stay in the Senate chamber. By the following morning it is on X, on Facebook, in the group chats of every Filipino family that has a member who used to work in BPO or send money from the Gulf. The red line going down. The shaded gap. The date 2032 at the end of a descent that the Philippine government’s own agency had drawn. Someone in the chamber had photographed it on a phone. Someone else had captioned it: ito na ang nawala sa atin. Within forty-eight hours the phrase, and the slide, are inseparable. Philippine media refer to it as “the DEPDev chart.” The opposition party uses it in every press statement for the next six months without attribution, because it does not need attribution. Everyone already knows which chart.

What makes the slide legible as a symbol is not the technical content. Nobody in the group chats is calculating $466 billion in foregone GDP. What they are reading is the shape: a line that was going up, and then was not. A dotted line showing where it was supposed to go, and a solid line showing where it went instead. The shaded area between them is not labeled in the original presentation. It does not need to be. The Inquirer editorial board, in its October 24 edition, labels it for the record: the cost of inaction. The Palace communications office issues a statement noting that the projection methodology is contested. What did not come back

The middle class. The cohort of 500,000–900,000 English-speaking, college-educated, internet-native workers who had anchored the Philippine middle class since the early 2000s no longer exists as an economic formation. Roughly 340,000 of them have emigrated, mostly to Canada, Australia, and the UAE, on skilled-worker tracks that will not renew after 2034. Another 610,000 have moved, in two waves, into informal-sector work at 40–60% of their previous real income. The PSA does not publish a line for this cohort because the PSA’s 2018 definitional framework did not anticipate a scenario in which this cohort contracted. In the absence of a statistic, there is no politics of recovery.

The Gulf corridor. DMW deployment of new domestic-worker contracts to Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Qatar, Kuwait, Bahrain, and Oman stands at 87,000 for 2031 — down 78% from 2025’s 398,000. The Gulf humanoid installed base, across domestic and logistics applications, crossed 2.4 million units in 2032. The crossover that arrived for Benjie in February 2029 has, by 2032, arrived for every Filipino OFW corridor below the registered-nurse wage floor. The ninety-day OFW return queue at NAIA Terminal 1, which had peaked at twenty-one days in November 2029, is now zero because there is no return wave left to queue.

The rice. The 2030 rice self-sufficiency print of 62.1% was, in retrospect, not a trough. It was a new baseline. The 2031 figure was 63.4%. The 2032 figure is tracking to 64.8%. Vietnam and Thailand, the two suppliers the Philippines had relied on for the import margin, signed bilateral volume guarantees with Saudi Arabia and Indonesia in 2030 and 2031 that compete directly with Philippine tender volumes. Nanay Tess, in Bayambang, pays ₱84 a kilo at the wet market in October 2032 for what had been ₱54 in January 2025. She does not complain because there is no one to complain to who is not also paying ₱84.

The grid. Luzon has logged 34 red alerts and 142 yellow alerts in 2032 through October. The Bataan Nuclear Power Plant rehabilitation, announced with much fanfare in 2031, entered a funding dispute with the Korean reactor vendor in August 2032 that had not, at quarter-end, been resolved. First criticality is now not expected before 2037. The two SMR memoranda of understanding signed in 2032 are memoranda of understanding. Neither has advanced to contract. Elijah, thirteen, has done homework by flashlight more often than not in the six weeks preceding 19 October.

The political ceiling. The president who won on labor sovereignty in 2028 is, in October 2032, completing her fourth year in office. She will govern until June 2034, when the next presidential election ends her term. The midterm verdict of 2031 — coalition losses in House and Senate, the economy-and-rice referendum — left her administration governing against a hostile legislature for her final three years. The Office for Philippine Labor Sovereignty, which had issued forty-one press releases and zero enforceable protections between 2028 and 2031, was consolidated into the DICT in the 2032 budget reconciliation. The ₱40-billion Philippine AI Infrastructure Fund, announced with San Miguel Energy in August 2028, has produced one deliverable: the 25-year Bataan Nuclear Power Plant power-supply concession awarded to San Miguel’s generation subsidiary in January 2029. The fund has not funded an AI deployment, a training program, or a displaced-worker benefit. It has funded a power deal. The concession runs through 2054. San Miguel’s energy division books a 14% operating margin in Q3 2032, its highest in six years.

The safety net. TESDA’s Transition Corps has trained 212,000 displaced workers against a displaced cohort of more than 1.5 million. Its placement rate at six months is 41%. Its placement rate at twenty-four months, previously reported at 71%, was revised to 52% in July 2032 after an internal audit found that 19 percentage points of the prior figure represented workers who had been placed and subsequently re-displaced within the follow-up window. The “4Ps” expansion from 4.4 to 6.2 million households, approved in principle in 2028, was deferred in 2030, deferred in 2031, and in 2032 is operating at 4.9 million households because the IMF program’s fiscal path does not permit more. There is no national AI Act. The 2029 bill died in committee and has not been reintroduced.

The 2032 floor, stated plainly

Crisis-scenario models in 2025 had forecast a 2030 trough followed by a gradual recovery phase. The trough arrived on schedule. The recovery did not. What arrived instead is stabilization at the trough — a permanent 40-plus-percent contraction of the BPO base, a permanent evaporation of the Gulf remittance corridor, a permanent peso at ₱80, a permanent inability to fund the safety net that the displacement demands. There is no scenario path, in the research dossier, in which 2035 looks materially better than 2032 without a policy shift that requires decisions to have been made in 2026. Those decisions were not made in 2026.

Scene · Mandaluyong, 19 October 2032, 7:40 p.m.

Benjie is thirty-two. He has been driving for Grab in Antipolo for twenty-nine months, at a real rate per ride that has fallen 34% since 2028 because the rider pool has quintupled. He no longer remits anything to Nanay Tess because he cannot. Nanay Tess, who is sixty-four, still lives in Bayambang on a teacher’s pension that — in real terms — now buys what two-thirds of that pension had bought in 2025. She has given up the landline. She reads the news on a secondhand phone Maria had sent her in 2030 and which, in October 2032, has a cracked screen Maria cannot afford to replace. The rice on the table tonight is from her cousin Rommel’s cooperative in Pangasinan, and it is the cheapest thing on the table, and she is proud of it in a way that, in 2025, she would not have understood.

Sophia, ten, asks Maria why her Tita Karen, who had lived in the next building over, no longer visits. Maria explains that Tita Karen lives in Calgary now. Sophia asks what Calgary is. Maria tells her it is a city in Canada. Sophia asks when they will visit. Maria does not answer.

Elijah, thirteen, is upstairs finishing a history assignment on the 1983 Ninoy assassination. The assignment prompt, generated by the DepEd learning platform, asks him to describe a moment at which the Philippines changed irreversibly. His first draft names 1983. His teacher, in the margin comments, will suggest that he consider 2026 instead. He does not know what happened in 2026 that would qualify. No one has explicitly told him. It has not been taught.

Maria pays the condo bill from her phone. The balance is ₱184,000 on a loan that, after three restructurings, will clear in August 2033. The condo, at current peripheral-condo-market prices, is worth ₱2.1 million against a remaining loan of ₱184,000 and a replacement cost in 2032 pesos of ₱4.8 million. She has been current on every payment since September 2026. This is the thing she will have built. It is the only thing she will have built.

Nothing ends cleanly. The country has settled into the shape it takes when the shocks run out of things to break. A third of the middle class has emigrated. Another third works informally at 40% of prior wages. The grid is unreliable, the rice is imported, the safety net covers less than half of the displaced, and the decisions that would have foreclosed this path were due in 2026 and were not made. None of this reverses on its own. There is no external actor coming to fix it. What 2032 is, is what 2032 will continue to be, for as long as the political system remains incapable of admitting what it is looking at.

The only hope that exists in this scenario exists in 2026

Everything described in chapters 02 through 07 was, in 2026, still reversible. The BPO contraction could have been cushioned by a displacement fund legislated in 2026 instead of 2028. The Gulf crossover could have been prepared for by a sovereign humanoid-policy framework in 2026 instead of 2029. The peso could have been defended from a stronger reserve position in 2028 instead of from a depleted one in 2030. The rice could have been protected by Vietnamese and Thai bilateral agreements signed in 2027 instead of lost to Saudi and Indonesian counter-signings in 2031. Every mechanism by which the 2032 floor could have been higher was visible in the 2025 research. The decisions were not made. The decisions have to be made — if this path is to be foreclosed — now. Not in 2030, when the peso is breaking. Now.

The reason the 2032 chapter is the way it is — stabilization at the trough, no recovery phase, a middle class that has emigrated or decomposed, a rice floor that does not rise, a grid that does not heal — is that the Philippines in this scenario has, uniquely among the exposed economies of 2026, no second stabilizer.
